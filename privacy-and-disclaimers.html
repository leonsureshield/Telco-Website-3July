Appendix A Legal Risk Considerations

This appendix summarizes the categories of legal risk the authors took into account in preparing cybersecurity-related risk information presented on the Sites, together with the corresponding controls applied. It is provided for transparency. Nothing in this appendix constitutes legal advice; readers requiring such advice should consult qualified counsel.

A.1 Computer Fraud & Abuse Act

Federal courts have at times interpreted the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act broadly, including in contexts involving analysis of publicly accessible information about computer systems. To remain well within accepted boundaries, the analysis underlying this report was conducted on a strictly non-intrusive basis. No authentication was attempted against any system. No access controls were circumvented. No proprietary, internal or non-public data was accessed or attempted. No system performance, availability or confidentiality was impacted. The work relied exclusively on openly accessible information and on widely-used reference sources, used in a manner consistent with the published terms governing those sources.

A.2 Digital Millennium Copyright Act §1201

Section 1201 of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act restricts circumvention of technological measures controlling access to copyrighted works. This report contains no exploit code, no bypass technique, and no actionable instruction that could be construed as enabling circumvention of any access control. References to widely-documented vulnerabilities, including CVEs in the CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue, are limited to identification by CVE number and to descriptions already in the public domain. Such references are presented exclusively at the industry-aggregate level.

A.3 Defamation & Reputational Harm

Sector-level cybersecurity reporting can give rise to reputational claims if specific operators are characterized in ways they consider inaccurate or harmful. To mitigate this risk, the report does not name any individual provider. All findings are aggregated and reported at the industry level. The report has been drafted to describe observable industry-level conditions — using terms such as “present,” “analyzed,” “publicly visible,” and “elevated risk band” — rather than to assert fault or negligence with respect to any operator.

A.4 Critical-Infrastructure Sensitivity

Telecommunications is a designated critical-infrastructure sector under the US Department of Homeland Security sector classification. To respect the sensitivity associated with that designation, sensitive technical detail has been intentionally generalized throughout this report. No IP address, asset-specific identifier, or operator-specific configuration appears in the report, and no exploitation pathway is described. The focus of the report is on aggregate sector posture relevant to policy, board-level and resilience discussion.

A.5 Tool & Source Terms of Use

The analysis underlying this report relied on openly accessible information accessed in a manner consistent with the published terms governing the relevant sources. Where automated retrieval was used, it was operated within the parameters those sources permit. Where reference tools or libraries were used, they were used in accordance with their respective licensing terms.

A.6 Regulatory & Methodological Framework Alignment

The approach taken in preparing this report is consistent in spirit with established frameworks for non-intrusive cybersecurity research, including the passive-assessment principles articulated in NIST Special Publication 800-115, the external attack-surface management principles reflected in CISA Binding Operational Directive 23-02, the best-practice guidance issued by the FCC Communications Security, Reliability, and Interoperability Council, and DHS guidelines for security research touching on critical-infrastructure sectors. References to such frameworks are made for context only and do not imply endorsement, accreditation, certification or approval by any of the referenced bodies.
