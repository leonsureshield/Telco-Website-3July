Why a Telecommunications Cybersecurity Report — and Why Now?

PreparedMay 2026
DATA SOURCESPublicly available OSINT & Information

Telecommunications is foundational critical national infrastructure - emergency services, government, finance, healthcare, energy, transport and public safety all depend on this key industry sector. The cybersecurity posture of any CNI industry sector is of public-interest, even before the commercial concerns for the businesses operating them.

Conventional industry cybersecurity posture reporting asks internal questions, such as controls deployed, systems patched, audits passed. Those matter at the operator level. They do not, in aggregate, describe what publicly available information shows about the sector as a whole, increasingly the lens through which regulators, boards and the public form their view. This report addresses the latter question, at the industry level, for an unusually fragmented sector with a long tail of small operators. Over half of the telecommunication operators can be classified as small to mid-size businesses (less than $10M in annual revenue).

Telecommunications Cybersecurity: Frequently Asked Questions

Direct answers to the questions most often asked about telecommunications cybersecurity, the 2026 report, and how telecom operators can reduce cyber risk.

Q1What is the US Telecommunications Sector 2026 Cyber Risks, Threats & Guidance report?+
It is an industry-first telecommunications cybersecurity report from SureShield and BorderHawk that analyses external cyber-risk indicators across 3,106 of the 7,320 US telecommunication operators. It scores composite risk by state and sector using only publicly available OSINT, and names no individual operator in the public report.
Q2Why is cybersecurity important in the telecommunications industry?+
Telecommunications is classified as critical national infrastructure — emergency services, government, finance, healthcare, energy, transport and public safety all depend on it. When telecom networks are compromised, everything downstream can be disrupted. That makes cybersecurity in the telecommunications industry a public-interest priority, not only a commercial one.
Q3What are the biggest cybersecurity threats to telecom operators in 2026?+
Leading telco cybersecurity threats include state-aligned espionage such as Salt Typhoon, ransomware, exploitation of known unpatched CVEs on edge devices, email spoofing from weak SPF and DMARC, and subdomain takeover from dangling DNS. Edge routers, firewalls and VPN appliances are frequent initial-access points.
Q4What are the most common telecommunications vulnerabilities?+
Across the 3,106 operators analysed, the most widespread telecommunications vulnerabilities are subdomain takeover risk (43%), presence on the CISA KEV catalogue (42%) and elevated EPSS exploit likelihood (35%). Weak email authentication and external attack-surface exposure are also common across the sector.
Q5How many US telecom operators are at elevated cyber risk?+
About 66% — 2,049 of the 3,106 operators analysed — sit in an elevated risk band (Critical or High) on the composite indicator framework. The mean composite score is 67% and the median is 79%, showing elevated risk is the sector norm, not a small group of underperformers.
Q6What is Salt Typhoon and why does it matter for telecom cybersecurity?+
Salt Typhoon is a sustained, state-aligned cyber-espionage campaign, publicly disclosed in late 2024, that compromised major US telecommunications networks for intelligence collection. Edge devices were the main entry point, often via known unpatched vulnerabilities. It is documented in CISA, FBI and NSA joint advisory AA25-239A (27 Aug 2025).
Q7What data sources were used in this telecom cybersecurity report?+
Only publicly available, non-intrusive sources: the FCC public registry, the CISA KEV catalogue, NIST and EPSS scoring references, Verizon DBIR and IBM CODB for industry context, public DNS and advisories, and passive OSINT collection (passive DNS, certificates and service discovery). No authenticated scans were performed and no access controls were circumvented.
Q8How is the telecom cyber-risk score calculated?+
Each operator is scored across five weighted pillars: Threats and Exploits Exposure from Vulnerabilities (30%), External Attack Surface Risk (25%), Risk from Infrastructure (20%), Risk from Web Applications (15%) and Email Security Risk (10%). Each pillar is normalised to 0–100 and combined into a composite, with critical signals such as KEV exploits and exposed secrets adding penalties.
Q9What is subdomain takeover risk and why is it common among telcos?+
Subdomain takeover happens when DNS records point to services that are no longer claimed (dangling DNS), letting an attacker control a subdomain. It affects 43% of analysed telecom operators — the single most widespread indicator — and is often invisible to operators internally.
Q10Are small telecom operators more exposed to cyber risk?+
The sector has a long tail of small operators: around 90% of FCC-registered entities are privately held, 52% operate below $10M revenue, and roughly 69% employ fewer than 50 people. These resource-constrained telcos often lack dedicated security teams, raising sector-wide cybersecurity concerns for telcos.
Q11Does this report name individual telecom operators?+
No. The public report presents aggregate, industry-level findings and names no individual operator. Detailed per-domain and sub-domain output is released only to verified representatives of the operator concerned, in line with responsible disclosure.
Q12How can a telecom operator reduce its cyber risk after the report?+
Operators can run a cybersecurity test on their own domain using the risk-profile lookup to get an aggregated band classification, then work through a Detect, Remediate, Verify and Sustain loop. SureShield's automated security and GRC platform and BorderHawk's advisory services help telecommunication operators turn these findings into measurable, lasting risk reduction.
