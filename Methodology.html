Composite Indicator Framework

Detailed Methodology

How the industry-level findings in The State of US Telecommunications were produced: the sources drawn on, the analytical base, the indicators measured, the composite scoring model, and the open-source tooling and attributions behind the analysis. Everything here operates strictly at the industry level. No individual provider is named.

PreparedMay 2026 BasisPublicly available OSINT LevelIndustry aggregate only
01 · Approach & Sources

An industry-level review built on public information

This report draws on publicly available information about the US telecommunication industry. The principal reference set is the FCC's public telecommunications provider registry, supplemented by openly published business intelligence on the providers identified in that registry and contextualized against established third-party industry research.

The objective is to characterize systemic cyber-risk signals affecting the sector at the industry level, with emphasis on the structural, economic and disclosure-driven dynamics relevant to policy and program oversight. No organization-level attribution is offered, and no individual provider is named.

02 · Data Sources

Only publicly accessible sources

The analysis uses open-source intelligence (OSINT) exclusively. No non-public, investigative or privileged data is used. Sources include:

  • The FCC public telecommunications provider registry
  • Government-maintained vulnerability repositories and advisories (for example, the CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog)
  • Security advisories issued by technology and equipment vendors
  • Public vulnerability disclosures aligned to common enumeration schemes (e.g. CVE), with EPSS and CVSS references
  • Publicly reported incidents, enforcement summaries and regulatory filings
  • Email-authentication posture (DMARC and SPF) as published in public DNS
  • Openly published industry research, including the Verizon DBIR series, IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report and CISA advisories

04 · Indicators

Widely-used industry concepts

The risk indicators referenced are widely-used industry concepts, not proprietary scores. Examples include the presence of CVEs cataloged by CISA as actively exploited, the posture of email-authentication records (DMARC and SPF) as published in DNS, dangling DNS configuration consistent with subdomain takeover risk, and the relative scale of an operator's public web and service surface.

These indicators are common reference points across multiple cybersecurity research publications, which makes the findings interpretable against the wider body of public research rather than against a single private model.

05 · The Composite Scoring Model

Five indicator categories, one weighted composite

Each pillar is normalized to a 0 to 100 scale, then combined into a weighted composite. Critical signals (KEV exploits, dangling DNS, exposed secrets, open databases) apply additional penalties on top of the weighted base. The composite places each provider into a risk band.

06 · Processing & Aggregation

Normalized, categorized, and aggregated

Collected disclosures are normalized to resolve duplication across sources, categorized by technology class or functional dependency, and evaluated over time to identify recurring patterns. Outputs are presented using aggregated statistics, ranges and trend indicators.

Geographic reporting is generalized where necessary, and organization-specific details are excluded from all public outputs. The methodology does not independently verify third-party disclosures or assess remediation status.

07 · Known Limitations

Directional indicators, not deterministic measures

Findings should be interpreted as directional indicators of systemic risk, not as deterministic measures of any provider's security posture. Three factors constrain the analysis:

  • Variability in disclosure practices across vendors and operators
  • Time lags between discovery, disclosure and public reporting
  • Absence of visibility into undisclosed vulnerabilities or mitigations
08 · Regulatory Context

Complementary to existing frameworks

This analysis complements, and does not replace, existing regulatory, grant-oversight and program frameworks. It highlights cross-cutting vulnerability conditions, informs guidance and technical-assistance strategies, and supports resilience considerations in telecommunications policy. It is not intended to establish new regulatory expectations or to interpret existing ones.

Findings should be read as industry-level observations rather than as assertions about any individual provider. Aggregate counts and percentages are intended to illuminate sector-wide patterns; they are not intended to enable, and should not be used to attempt, the identification of any specific operator.

Methodology Note

This report deliberately remains at the industry level. It does not name individual providers, does not attempt to characterize the security posture of any specific operator, and does not describe the technical mechanics by which industry-level indicators were compiled. It does not constitute, and should not be construed as, a vulnerability assessment, penetration test, security audit or compliance evaluation of the sector or of any provider within it. Readers seeking provider-specific information should approach providers directly.
